1. PROJECT SNAPSHOT
--------------------------------
Design 15 hi-fidelity mobile screens for 6ix AM Club — a
running-community app for a real club based in Pretoria,
South Africa. The club currently operates via Instagram,
WhatsApp, and Quicket (ticketing). The app unifies all three
into a single member experience.

This is a portfolio case study, not a shipping product. Prioritize
visual impact, brand cohesion, and storytelling.

Deliver: 15 screens at 390 × 844 (iPhone 15 Pro), both
LIGHT and DARK mode variants.

3. BRAND DNA
--------------------------------
Mission
"More than a running club — a community built on discipline,
consistency, and positive energy. From beginners to experienced
runners, everyone is welcome as we chase fitness, friendship,
and greatness one step at a time."

Four principles
01. RAW > POLISHED — Grain, blur, imperfection. Lived-in, not clinical.
02. EARLY > EVERYWHERE — Pre-dawn aesthetic. Dark by default.
03. LOCAL > GLOBAL — Pretoria-specific, Jack Budha-rooted. Not generic Strava.
04. COMMUNITY > COMPETITION — Leaderboards reward showing up, not elite pace.

Voice
WE SAY:
- "See you at 6."
- "5K. Free to join."
- "Show up. That's the whole plan."
- "Pretoria's morning crew."

WE DON'T SAY:
- "Crush your PR with AI-powered insights."
- "Unlock premium features."
- "Gamified fitness journey."
- "Level up your wellness."

- Geometric with rounded/bulbous terminals
- Condensed proportions
- Modernist, almost display-stencil quality

11. CASE STUDY NARRATIVE (for portfolio)
--------------------------------

TITLE
Turning a WhatsApp group and an Instagram page into a brand.

SUBTITLE
A product concept for 6ix AM Club — Pretoria's morning running
community — unifying three fragmented platforms into one
membership experience.

ROLE
UX & UI Design, Brand Extension, Prototype
(Solo project, 2026)

THE PROBLEM
6ix AM Club is real. Every Saturday at 6AM, a group of runners
meets at Jack Budha in Pretoria for a 5K. The club has a growing
following, a strong visual identity, and a dedicated WhatsApp
channel. But members have to jump across three platforms to
stay involved: Instagram for announcements, WhatsApp for
day-to-day chatter, and Quicket for ticketing. The aesthetic
experience is tight on IG but completely broken everywhere else.

THE QUESTION
How do you turn a scattered community into a single product
experience — without losing what made it intimate in the
first place?

THE APPROACH
I treated the existing brand as the source of truth. The
Vol.02 poster already defined the visual language: motion
blur, editorial typography, a single orange accent, and raw
community portraits. My job wasn't to invent a brand — it
was to extend one into a digital product.

I built in three layers:
1. Design system — tokens distilled from the posters
2. Information architecture — five core tabs derived from
   actual user behavior (runs, events, feed, board, me)
3. Product flows — four end-to-end journeys anchored in real
   club moments (signing up, booking a run, tracking a run,
   engaging with the community)

KEY DECISIONS
- Dark-first UI. The club runs at 6AM in the dark — the
  product should feel like the moment.
- Orange as scarcity. The route-orange from the existing
  flyers stays reserved for primary actions and live-run
  indicators only. Overuse would flatten it.
- Quicket as co-host. Rather than replacing the club's
  existing ticketing platform, the app embeds Quicket
  checkout with brand-aligned styling. Product thinking,
  not product ego.
- Community, not competition. Leaderboards are sorted by
  consistency (streaks, attendance) alongside distance —
  mirroring the club's actual value system.

OUTCOME
15 hi-fidelity screens covering four user flows, in both
light and dark modes, with a full design system extensible
to future features (matchmaking, coaching, multi-chapter
support).

WHAT I LEARNED
The hardest part wasn't the pixels — it was deciding what
to leave out. A running app could easily become a Strava
clone. Keeping it specific to this club, this city, and
this 6AM ritual is what makes it a product, not a template.
